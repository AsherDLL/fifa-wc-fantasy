# 11g - Minutes, captaincy, and the variance the point model cannot see

Status: **DRAFT**

Section 11f fixed the predictive core (form feature plus WC retraining)
and the per-position ensemble. This section addresses the three gaps that
section listed as still open: minutes and rotation, captaincy, and the
goalkeeper defensive signal. Two of the three produced usable
improvements. One produced a clean negative result that is worth keeping
in the record. The section closes with an analysis of the round-to-round
scoring oscillation the operator observed, which turns out to be the same
phenomenon the captaincy fix exploits.

The A/B/C/D RMSE table is regenerated by
`scripts/wc_forward_validation.py`. The availability-discount,
captaincy, and GK-blend experiments were run as session analyses whose
harnesses are described in the text below, against the realised
per-round data in `data/user_squads/` plus `data/raw/players_*.parquet`;
converting those decision-layer experiments into committed scripts is
an open item in Section 11h.

## 11g.1 Minutes and rotation: right problem, wrong layer

A benched starter scores the appearance floor at best and zero at worst,
regardless of form or matchup. The team-news pipeline (Section 11e) was
supposed to supply predicted lineups, but it returns zero predicted-XI
records per tick because the soccerdata proxy has no World Cup coverage.
So the minutes signal had to come from data we already hold.

The World Cup player table does not expose per-round minutes, but it does
expose `round_points`, one realised total per completed round. A player
who took the pitch banks at least the appearance points, so
participation is proxied by `round_points > 0`. `start_rate_lag` is the
trailing fraction of a player's last three completed rounds with a
positive score. On the EPL side the same feature uses the real `starts`
flag. Both are leak-free (shifted by one match) and share one definition
(`training/features.add_lagged_start_rate`,
`features/build._attach_start_rate_lag`, `training/wc.py`).

The signal is real. Bucketed over all WC player-rounds:

| Recent participation | n | mean realised pts | P(scores 0) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 to 33 percent | 613 | 1.67 | 0.36 |
| 34 to 66 percent | 473 | 2.50 | 0.19 |
| ever-present (100 percent) | 1526 | 3.07 | 0.13 |

An ever-present player is worth nearly double a rotation risk and blanks
a third as often. The correlation of `start_rate_lag` with realised
points is 0.171.

The instinct is to feed this to the GBM. That instinct is wrong. Adding
`start_rate_lag` (with `team_gc_form`, below) to the GBM feature set
regressed the leak-free WC walk-forward RMSE at every position (config D
versus config C, pooled 3.281 to 3.307). Over four rounds the WC
participation proxy is too noisy, and it is collinear with `form_lag`
(a benched player already has low form), so it adds variance the tree
cannot use.

The correct layer is the optimiser, not the model. The minutes signal is
a selection adjustment: discount a player's effective points toward a
floor by their participation rate, so the squad solver stops spending on
rotation risks. `optimizer/pipeline.apply_availability_discount` scales
effective points by `floor + (1 - floor) * start_rate_lag`, with the
floor at 0.5 (never zero, because the proxy is imperfect and a nailed
starter can blank twice). Validated on the clean heuristic backend across
MD1 to R32, the discount lifted realised squad points from 275 to 283,
and the result was stable for any floor in [0.3, 0.7]. It also surfaces a
`rotation_risk` flag for the report. Eight points over four rounds is
small, but for a team at the bottom of the table every point is the
point.

## 11g.2 Goalkeepers: a clean negative result

The hypothesis from Section 11f was that personal scoring form is noise
for goalkeepers and the right signal is team defensive form. The first
half is correct. The second is not, at least not at World Cup sample
sizes.

`team_gc_form` is the trailing mean of goals conceded by a player's team
(`features/build.team_gc_history`, leak-free, shared by inference and WC
label extraction). It was tested two ways:

1. As a GBM feature (config D above): regressed GK RMSE from 3.350 to
   3.380 pooled, and every other position too.
2. Blended into the Poisson clean-sheet term, which is where goalkeeper
   points actually come from. Sweeping the blend weight on 221 held-out
   GK rows, RMSE rose monotonically with the weight (3.48 at weight 0.00
   of the simplified formula to 3.73 at weight 1.00). More realised
   defensive form made the goalkeeper prediction worse, not better.

The reason is the same one that sinks personal GK form: three or four
matches of realised goals-conceded is a tiny, high-variance sample, and
it is dominated by opponent strength, which the structural model already
encodes. The structural Poisson backend remains the best goalkeeper
predictor (Section 07), and the ensemble already routes goalkeepers to
it. We are not shipping a defensive-form signal for goalkeepers. The
honest finding is that at this sample size no realised-form signal beats
structure for the position, and pretending otherwise would have cost
accuracy.

## 11g.3 Captaincy: captain the ceiling, not the mean

Captaincy doubles one player. Section 08b recorded that no backend picked
the optimal captain in any round, and the lineup solver
(`solvers.solve_lineup`) chose the captain as the argmax of mean
predicted points. Mean-argmax is the wrong objective for a team that is
behind. When you are chasing, you do not need the highest expected
return, you need the highest ceiling, because only a spike closes a gap.

The GBM already emits a q90 head. It was computed and then ignored by the
captain choice. A leak-free per-round backtest (train on WC rounds before
k, predict round k, take the top-eleven-by-mean as the XI) compared three
rules on raw captain points captured across MD2, MD3 and R32:

| Captain rule | raw captain points captured |
|---|---|
| mean-argmax (the shipped rule) | 11 |
| 0.5 mean + 0.5 q90 | 15 |
| q90-argmax (pure ceiling) | 21 |
| optimal (perfect hindsight) | 42 |

Captaining by ceiling captured nearly double the points of mean-argmax.
Doubled in scoring, that is a swing of roughly ten realised points over
three rounds, six to seven per round, for a team that has been losing
rounds by less.

`optimizer/captain.py` already had a composite selector that nobody had
wired in. It now has a ceiling-versus-mean base term,
`base = (1 - chase) * mean + chase * q90`, where `chase` is the
standings percentile (0 for the leader, 1 for last). A leader captains
the safe mean; a team in last captains the ceiling. The optimiser
(`optimizer/__main__`) now calls the selector to override the solver's
naive pick, defaulting `chase` to 0.9 because the operator is near the
bottom. For point-estimate backends with no q90, the base degrades to
the mean and nothing breaks.

## 11g.4 The 40-80 oscillation is variance, and variance is the plan

The operator observed a pattern: one round in the 40s, the next in the
80s, repeating. The realised starter totals (captain double excluded)
were 32, 75, 39, 73. The pattern is real in the data. The question is
whether it is a controllable cycle or something else.

It is not a cycle. It is variance, and the decisive evidence is that MD1
and MD2 used the identical eleven starters and scored 32 then 75. Same
players, no transfers, output more than doubled. Fantasy points come from
goals and assists, which are low-count Poisson events; an eleven-player
squad naturally swings between roughly 35 and 75 from one match to the
next even with selection held constant.

The point model cannot see this. Summing the heuristic's per-round
predictions for the operator's actual starters gives 60, 63, 49, 64: a
near-flat mean with a standard deviation of 5.8, against a realised
standard deviation of 19.4. A mean predictor is structurally incapable of
reproducing a goals-or-nothing swing, which is exactly why the q90 head
and the ceiling-aware captain matter.

There is a partly predictable component. The correlation between a
round's realised total and the average fixture strength gap of the
operator's starters is 0.48, and the correlation with the model's own
per-round prediction is 0.67. The high rounds tended to be the
easier-fixture rounds, and the model ranked them somewhat correctly even
though it understated the amplitude.

The forward-looking consequence for the quarter-final, semi-final and
final, with the operator chasing, is the opposite of smoothing the swing.
Variance is an asset when you are behind: a low-variance strategy locks in
a deficit. The remaining rounds should be played for ceiling. Concretely:

- Captain by q90, which the optimiser now does by default at the
  operator's standings.
- Time aggression to the rounds where the squad's own fixture strength
  gap is largest (the model's per-round squad prediction, at correlation
  0.67, is a usable ex-ante ranker of which upcoming round is the high
  one).
- Prefer low-ownership differentials in those rounds; a template captain
  who hauls converts no rank gain, a differential who hauls does.
- Do not spend transfers to reduce round-to-round variance. For a
  chaser, the variance is the only thing that can win it.

## 11g.5 Scorecard for this section

| Gap | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Minutes / rotation | Fixed at the optimiser layer (availability discount, +8 pts over four rounds). Rejected as a GBM feature (negative result). |
| Goalkeeper defensive form | Not shipped. Clean negative result: no realised-form signal beats structural Poisson for GK at WC sample sizes. |
| Captaincy | Fixed. Ceiling-aware selector wired into the optimiser, captured 21 versus 11 raw captain points on the leak-free backtest. |
| Scoring oscillation | Explained. Inherent per-match variance, partly fixture-driven (corr 0.48), to be exploited not smoothed while chasing. |

Two fixes, one honest negative result, and one phenomenon reclassified
from a mystery into the strategy. The negative result is not a failure of
the work; it is the work refusing to ship a signal that our own
measurements say makes the system worse.
